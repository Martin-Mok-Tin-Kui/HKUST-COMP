Wolfram's Search Goal: Compute All


By BRIAN DEAGON, INVESTOR'S BUSINESS DAILY
Posted 09/23/2009 06:11 PM ET

Stephen Wolfram's goal is simple enough to state: Take all the knowledge in the world and convert it into a computable format.

Hard to do.

His delivery vehicle is WolframAlpha.com, a Web site launched in May with some fanfare — to a somewhat perplexed public. WolframAlpha was misperceived by many as being a traditional search engine like Google (GOOG). It's not.

In Wolfram's parlance, it's a computational knowledge engine. Massive amounts of data have been collected and transformed into computable formats. WolframAlpha.com computes data, often presenting query results in the form of lists, charts and graphs.

Wolfram developed Mathematica, a popular computational software program used in the fields of math, engineering and science. It's sold by his privately held company, Wolfram Research.

Stephen Wolfram spoke with IBD about his knowledge engine site.

IBD: Who uses WolframAlpha and what are they doing with it?

Wolfram: There is a huge diversity of things that people are interested in, from technical things to business-oriented things to popular culture. I've been encouraged that people seem to understand what the point is. That is, you get to ask WolframAlpha specific questions and it provides specific answers, rather than asking about some general topic and expecting it will do what search engines do, giving you a bunch of links about that topic.

IBD: How much different is WolframAlpha from Google?

Wolfram: What we are trying to do is vastly more ambitious. We can take the knowledge that humans have accumulated and can make the whole thing computable. So that when someone asks a question, we can dip into that base of knowledge and compute a specific answer, as opposed to providing (links to) Web pages.

Our standard is higher. We're trying to give a specific answer.

IBD: Sometimes WolframAlpha doesn't give an answer. (The search engine states: "WolframAlpha isn't sure what to do with your input.") Why is that?

Wolfram: Half of the time it's because it doesn't understand the linguistics of the question. But our rate of being able to successfully answer questions is now above 75%. When we don't (have an answer), the good news, in a sense, is that half the time we do have the underlying data to answer the question.

This problem of enhancing the linguistic understanding capabilities is one that we have been making steady progress on. When WolframAlpha fails, the reasons are sometimes utterly banal, like people using a nickname for a city that WolframAlpha just doesn't happen to know yet. The No. 1 cause of failures is spelling errors.

IBD: Is WolframAlpha popular among investors?

Wolfram: I'm not the person who studies that in detail as others (within the company) do, but I know there has been lots of interest. When we look at queries, it's clear there is a lot of interest in the investment and financial area. We've worked hard developing that area. Our raw data is quite good and getting progressively better.

One thing coming soon is elaborate stock screening capabilities. We have two ends to that challenge. One is having the computation happen fast enough, the other is having a good enough linguistic understanding of all the elaborate ways that people specify conditions about stocks.

IBD: What's inside WolframAlpha?

Wolfram: We're approaching 7 million lines of mathematic code. It's a little hard to quantify. I can tell you how much code is inside. If you ask how much knowledge is there, there is no particularly good way to quantify that. One of the big projects is to curate data and make it computable. The challenge there is to find really reliable sources of data. Going from the raw table of numbers to the full thing — that is, setting it up so that it can be computed — is a big challenge. About 98% of the time the data you get from WolframAlpha is internal to our computer systems. There are a few cases where it's real time data. Stock quotes are one example.

IBD: What are the long-term plans for WolframAlpha?

Wolfram: Our long-term goal is to make as much knowledge that's been accumulated in the world computable as possible. That's going to take a really long time. There are some different capabilities we are working on. That includes, for example, the ability to upload your own data to WolframAlpha and have it do analysis on that data.

Another direction we are just starting to play with — it's still in the category of seeming impossible, but I'm guessing that within a limited number of years we will be able to do some pretty interesting things — is being able to invent on the fly. You might ask WolframAlpha to invent an electric circuit that has the following characteristics. Or invent a way to combine different financial instruments to achieve some particular objective. You could ask for information such as Asian countries ranked by GDP. You'll be able to ask that and get your spreadsheet populated with that data immediately. You can expect us to be able to do that with other types of software programs, like computer automated design.

IBD: That implies licensing deals with software companies, yes?

Wolfram: Our basic approach is to have many licensing deals, and many interesting things will come out of that in due course. I'm impressed at how many companies and organizations have self-identified what they can do with our technology.

And now the challenge is to take all the deals that make the most sense and to set the technology up so that everything can flow through.

IBD: Do you have deals in place?

Wolfram: All sorts of interesting things have happened, but none are announced as of yet.

IBD: How is WolframAlpha being funded?

Wolfram: This is my crazy project, so I get to pay for it. (Wolfram Research) has been used for our seed development capital.

There are other deals that have helped fuel us going forward. (And there are ads on the site.)

IBD: What motivates you?

Wolfram: Most people know that all sorts of things can be done with science, but they themselves can't get access to those capabilities. So I get the satisfaction of being able to take those achievements and package them in a way so that anyone can get immediate access to them, and democratize the access.
